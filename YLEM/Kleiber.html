Beverly Kleiber aka Reiser

As a pioneer in the field of Electronic Art, Beverly Kleiber (aka Beverly Reiser) was at the forefront of the exploration of new technologies creating immersive and engaging poetic experiences, As an early adopter, Kleiber's artistic journey is characterized by her embrace of digital media and her ability to push the boundaries of interactive art. Her work in the 1990s was on the cusp of electronic art that was just beginning to take shape, allowing her to contribute significantly to the development and evolution of these nascent art forms.

As part of the Life on a Slice collective, Kleiber co-developed interactive computer/video installations that explored decision-making processes. These installations provide an environmental metaphor for decisions based "slices of information." In Come Human, Spin in my Web, the participant takes a ride through an A.I.'s persona. An A.I. sends an invitation out to the Humans to visit the "Web". If they take up the invite, they adventure into either the "Cerebral Cathedral" or the "Feral Forest". Eventually the participant arrives at one of 4 possible endings having chosen to either alter their own "data structure" or the environment.

The interactive CDROM "Voice Garden: Labyrinth of Love, Labyrinth of Desire explores themes of love and desire through interactive narrative and multimedia elements.

In 1995, Beverly and Lucia Grossberger Morales presented their altar to Remedios Varo, the surrealist painter (1908-1963) in Que Viva Tu Recuerdo: Altars and Offerings for the Days of the Dead at the Oakland Museum of California. Varo's paintings portray female alchemists, mysticism, and science. They believed that an electronic altar would best represent Varos since Varo was a Surrealist and the characters in her paintings emerged magically from walls or floors. With a computer, characters emerge with a feeling of magic out of nothingness.

The altar consists of photos of Remedios Varo and her work projected onto a large scrim on each side are electronic candles that reflect the image of the person at the altar. Sounds from a forest are interrupted by Varo's stream of consciousness writing.

Beverly created an immersive installation, Vital Journey / Virtual Heart, at the children's science museum in Kansas. This exhibit invites visitors to embark on a virtual journey through the human circulatory system, lungs, and brain. It utilized life-size video capture and interactive game technology to engage the audiences.

At the San Francisco Art Institute's McBean Gallery, Kleiber exhibited private_loves/public_opera, a video "garden" where love stories from both online and in person participants were collected. They could read or contribute their own love stories, creating an interactive narrative experience using the Internet and the Ylem website.

Beverly Kleiber as the president of YLEM, Artists Using Science and Technology, from 1985 to 1999. She provided opportunities for dialog between artists, scientists, authors, curators, educators and art enthusiasts to explored this vibrant and promising intersection. Beverly curated several electronic art exhibitions, including Ubiquitous Art Zone at SIGCHI (Special Interest Group Computer Human Interface.) Her memoir:" Ylem/ Artists Using Science and Technology in the Wildernes of Art in the 1990s was published by The Leonardo Journal, MIT Press.

Kleiber's early work included sculptures incorporating neon, sandblasted mirrors, and stained glass. Her large-scale stained-glass window titled Birth, Growth and Return, will be permanently installed in the San Francisco Zen Center's Enso Village.

At her core, Beverly was a playful instigator living and working on the cusp of new paradigms in Art and technology.
